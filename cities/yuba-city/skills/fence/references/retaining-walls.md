# Retaining Wall Requirements and Engineering Triggers

Source: 2025 California Building Code (Title 24, Part 2), Chapter 18, Section 1807 — Foundation Walls, Retaining Walls, and Embedded Posts and Poles; California Residential Code (Part 2.5), Section R105.2 — Permits.

---

## Permit and Engineering Thresholds

### When Is a Permit Required?

| Condition | Permit Required? | Code Section |
|---|---|---|
| Retaining wall <=4 ft (footing to top), no surcharge | NO (exempt) | CRC R105.2, item 3 |
| Retaining wall >4 ft (footing to top) | YES | CRC R105.2 |
| Retaining wall supporting surcharge (any height) | YES | CRC R105.2 |
| Retaining wall impounding flammable liquids (any height) | YES | CRC R105.2 |

**Height measurement:** Measured from the **bottom of the footing** to the **top of the wall**. This is NOT the exposed face height — it includes the below-grade footing depth.

### When Is Engineering Required?

A registered design professional (California licensed civil engineer, structural engineer, or architect) is typically required when:

| Condition | Engineering Required? |
|---|---|
| Height >4 ft (footing to top) | Yes |
| Any height with surcharge loads | Yes — must quantify surcharge |
| Adverse soil conditions (expansive, liquefiable, fill) | Yes — soils report required |
| Slope above or below wall (sloping backfill or toe) | Typically yes |
| Tiered/stepped walls within influence zone of each other | Yes |
| Walls supporting structures, driveways, or utilities | Yes |
| Walls adjacent to public right-of-way | Yes (city requirement) |
| Walls on or near property lines | Often yes (city requirement) |

**Surcharge loads** include:
- Vehicles or driveways above the wall
- Buildings or structures above the wall
- Sloping ground above the wall
- Stored materials or equipment
- Adjacent walls within the influence zone (typically 2x the wall height)

## Structural Design Requirements (CBC 1807.2)

### General (1807.2.1)
Retaining walls must be designed to ensure stability against:
- **Overturning** about the toe
- **Sliding** along the base
- **Excessive foundation pressure** on the bearing soil
- **Water uplift** (hydrostatic pressure)

### Lateral Soil Loads (1807.2.2)
Retaining walls must be designed for the lateral soil loads per CBC Section 1610.

**Typical equivalent fluid pressures:**

| Soil Type | At-Rest Pressure (psf/ft) | Active Pressure (psf/ft) |
|---|---|---|
| Sand / gravel (well-drained) | 55 | 30 |
| Sand / silt mix | 65 | 45 |
| Clay / silt (poorly drained) | 85 | 60 |

**For SDC D, E, or F:** Retaining walls supporting more than 6 ft of backfill must incorporate additional seismic lateral earth pressure per geotechnical investigation (CBC 1807.2.2).

### Safety Factors (1807.2.3)

| Condition | Minimum Safety Factor |
|---|---|
| Sliding resistance (without seismic) | 1.5 |
| Overturning resistance (without seismic) | 1.5 |
| Sliding resistance (with seismic) | 1.1 |
| Overturning resistance (with seismic) | 1.1 |

The safety factor against lateral sliding = available soil resistance at base / net lateral force applied.

Design is based on:
- 0.7 times nominal earthquake loads
- 1.0 times other nominal loads
- Investigation with one or more variable loads set to zero

The standard load combinations of CBC Section 1605 do NOT apply to retaining wall stability calculations.

### Segmental Retaining Walls (1807.2.4)
Dry-cast concrete units for segmental retaining walls must comply with **ASTM C1372**. These are gravity walls relying on mass, batter, and geogrid reinforcement rather than rebar/concrete.

## Retaining Wall Types

### Gravity Walls
- Rely on self-weight to resist overturning
- Typically masonry, stone, or mass concrete
- Practical height limit: approximately 3-4 ft
- No reinforcement required (mass provides stability)
- Common for short residential walls and garden walls

### Cantilever Walls
- Reinforced concrete "L" or "T" shaped
- Most common for heights 4-12 ft
- Heel extends behind the wall under the retained soil — weight of soil on heel helps resist overturning
- Requires engineering for rebar sizing, footing design
- Typical stem thickness: 8-12 inches
- Typical footing width: 0.5 to 0.7 times the wall height

### Counterfort / Buttress Walls
- Cantilever walls with vertical ribs (counterforts) on the soil side or buttresses on the exposed side
- Used for heights over 12-15 ft
- Requires engineering

### Segmental / Modular Block Walls
- Interlocking precast concrete blocks (e.g., Allan Block, Keystone, Versa-Lok)
- Typically battered (leaning into the slope)
- Geogrid reinforcement layers for walls over 3-4 ft
- Must comply with ASTM C1372
- Manufacturer provides design tables or engineering
- Drainage aggregate behind wall is critical

### Soldier Pile / Lagging Walls
- Steel H-piles or pipe piles with wood or concrete lagging between
- Used for deep excavations and temporary shoring
- Requires engineering

## Drainage Requirements

Drainage behind retaining walls is critical to prevent hydrostatic pressure buildup, which can double or triple the lateral load on the wall.

### Drainage Methods

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Weep holes** | 4" diameter PVC pipes through wall face at 6-8 ft OC horizontally, installed at or near the base. Minimum 2 per wall section. Screen the inlet side to prevent clogging. |
| **Perforated drain pipe** | 4" perforated PVC or corrugated pipe behind wall at base, sloped minimum 1% to daylight outlet. Wrapped in filter fabric sock or surrounded by drain rock with filter fabric. |
| **Drain rock / gravel fill** | 12" minimum width of clean drain rock (3/4" to 1-1/2") behind wall from base to within 12" of top. Filter fabric between drain rock and native soil to prevent soil migration. |
| **Surface drainage** | Grade the top of the backfill to slope away from the wall (minimum 2% slope for 10 ft). Swale or berm at top of slope to divert surface water. |

### Drainage Best Practices
- **Always** provide drainage behind retaining walls — lack of drainage is the most common cause of retaining wall failure
- In expansive clay soils, positive drainage is essential to prevent soil swelling
- Do not rely solely on weep holes — they can clog
- Perforated drain pipe + drain rock + filter fabric is the preferred system
- Outlet the drain pipe to daylight, a storm drain, or a sump — never into a sewer line
- Waterproofing or dampproofing the back face of the wall is recommended but does not substitute for drainage

## Guards at Retaining Walls (CBC 1807.2.5)

### When Required (1807.2.5.1)
Guards are required at retaining walls that are:
- Located within **36 inches** (914 mm) of a walking surface, AND
- Where the walking surface is more than **30 inches** (762 mm) above the surface or grade below at any point within 36 inches horizontally of the open side

**Exception:** Guards are not required at retaining walls not accessible to the public.

### Height (1807.2.5.2)
- Residential (CRC R321.1.2): minimum **42 inches** above the adjacent walking surface
- Commercial (CBC 1015.3): minimum **42 inches** above the adjacent walking surface

### Opening Limitations (1807.2.5.3)
- Guard openings must not allow passage of a **4-inch diameter sphere** (CBC 1015.4 / CRC R321.1.3)
- Triangular openings at stair guards: 6-inch sphere maximum

### Guard Loads
- Guards must resist a concentrated load of **200 pounds** applied in any direction at the top (CRC R301.5)
- Guard infill components: **50 pounds** per square foot

### Practical Application
When a fence is placed on top of a retaining wall and serves as the guard:
- The fence must meet guard height requirements (42" above the walking surface at the top of the wall)
- The fence must meet guard opening limitations (4" sphere test)
- The fence must resist the 200 lb concentrated load at the top
- A standard 6 ft privacy fence on a retaining wall typically satisfies these requirements if properly constructed

## Embedded Posts and Poles (CBC 1807.3)

For fence posts and sign posts embedded directly in earth or concrete footings:

### Design Criteria (1807.3.2)

**Nonconstrained posts** (no lateral constraint at ground surface):
- Depth determined by Equation 18-1 in CBC 1807.3.2.1
- Depends on: applied lateral force (P), allowable lateral soil bearing pressure (S1), post/footing diameter (b), and height of force application (h)

**Constrained posts** (lateral constraint at ground surface, e.g., rigid slab):
- Depth determined by Equation 18-2 or 18-3 in CBC 1807.3.2.2
- Constraint significantly reduces required embedment depth

### Backfill Methods (1807.3.3)

| Method | Specification |
|---|---|
| Concrete backfill | f'c >= 2,000 psi; hole minimum 4" larger than post |
| Sand backfill | Clean sand, tamped in layers not over 8" deep |
| CLSM (flowable fill) | Controlled low-strength material |

### Limitations (1807.3.1)
- Posts embedded in earth shall not provide lateral support for plaster, masonry, or concrete unless bracing is provided
- Wood poles must be pressure-treated per AWPA U1, Use Category 4B

## Prescriptive Retaining Wall Guidelines (<=4 ft, No Surcharge)

For short retaining walls that do not require a permit or engineering, these prescriptive guidelines represent common practice:

### Concrete Masonry Retaining Walls (<=4 ft)

| Component | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Wall thickness | 8" CMU minimum |
| Vertical rebar | #4 @ 24" OC |
| Horizontal rebar | #4 in bond beam at top and every 24" OC |
| Footing width | 24" minimum (3x wall thickness) |
| Footing depth | 12" minimum |
| Footing below grade | 12" minimum on low side |
| Footing rebar | 2 - #4 continuous, top and bottom |
| Dowels | #4 @ 24" OC, matching vertical rebar |
| Drainage | Drain rock + perforated pipe + filter fabric required |

### Poured Concrete Retaining Walls (<=4 ft)

| Component | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Stem thickness | 6" minimum |
| Stem vertical rebar | #4 @ 12" OC (soil side) |
| Stem horizontal rebar | #4 @ 18" OC |
| Footing width | 24"-30" |
| Footing depth | 10"-12" |
| Footing rebar | #4 @ 12" OC each way, top and bottom |
| Key | 4" wide x 4" deep key in bottom of footing (optional, aids sliding resistance) |

**These are guidelines only.** Actual requirements depend on soil conditions, surcharge, seismic zone, and local jurisdiction standards. When in doubt, require engineering.

## Common Retaining Wall Failures

Understanding failure modes helps identify plan review concerns:

| Failure Mode | Cause | Prevention |
|---|---|---|
| **Overturning** | Insufficient footing width, too much backfill height | Wider footing, shorter wall, reduce surcharge |
| **Sliding** | Insufficient friction at base, saturated soil | Key in footing, adequate drainage, roughened base |
| **Bearing failure** | Soil cannot support wall + backfill weight | Soils report, deeper footing, larger footing |
| **Structural failure** | Insufficient reinforcement, poor grout consolidation | Adequate rebar, proper grouting and inspection |
| **Drainage failure** | Hydrostatic pressure buildup behind wall | Always provide drain rock + pipe + filter fabric |
| **Settlement** | Wall on fill, poor compaction, expansive soil | Build on native soil or engineered fill |
| **Erosion/scour** | Water undermining footing at toe | Protect toe with riprap, concrete, or drainage |
