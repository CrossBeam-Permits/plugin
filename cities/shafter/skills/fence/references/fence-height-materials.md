# Fence Height Limits, Materials, and Installation

Source: 2025 California Residential Code (Title 24, Part 2.5), Section R105.2 — Permits; Section R304 — Protection of Wood; general industry standards for fence construction.

---

## Permit Exemption Thresholds

### State Code (CRC R105.2)

The California Residential Code exempts the following from building permit requirements:

| Structure | Exempt Threshold | Code Section |
|---|---|---|
| Fences | Not over 7 feet (2134 mm) high | CRC R105.2, Building item 2 |
| Retaining walls | Not over 4 feet (1219 mm) from bottom of footing to top of wall, unless supporting a surcharge | CRC R105.2, Building item 3 |

**Critical note:** This exemption is from the *state* code only. Most California cities adopt lower thresholds:
- Many cities require permits for fences over **6 feet**
- Some cities require permits for any fence within a certain distance of the property line (e.g., 3 ft)
- Front yard fences are often limited to 3-4 ft by city zoning code regardless of permit
- Always check the applicable city overlay skill

The permit exemption does NOT exempt the work from complying with code. Even a permit-exempt fence must meet all applicable code requirements.

## Height Measurement Rules

### General Rule
Fence and wall height is measured **vertically** from the **finished grade** at the base of the fence to the top of the fence (or top of any cap, finial, or post extension).

### Fences on Slopes
On sloping terrain, height is typically measured from the **higher adjacent finished grade**. The method of stepping the fence (racked panels vs. stepped panels) affects how height is measured:
- **Racked panels:** Panels follow the slope; height measured perpendicular to grade
- **Stepped panels:** Panels are level, stepping down the slope; height measured from the high side of each panel

### Fences on Top of Retaining Walls
When a fence is placed on top of a retaining wall, the two structures interact:
- **Total height** is commonly measured from the **lower finished grade** (the grade the retaining wall retains)
- Many cities limit the combined height (retaining wall + fence) to 6 ft from the lower grade
- The fence portion alone must also comply with fence height limits measured from the top of the retaining wall (upper grade)
- This is primarily regulated by city zoning code — state code does not specifically address combined height

### Post Extensions
If fence posts extend above the top rail or panel, most jurisdictions measure to the **top of the post**. Decorative post caps and finials are typically included in the height measurement.

## Material Types and Requirements

### Wood Fences

**Common wood fence types:**
- Board-on-board (privacy)
- Dog-ear / flat-top (privacy)
- Shadowbox / alternating board (semi-privacy)
- Picket (open)
- Lattice top (privacy with open top)
- Split rail (open, rural)

**Wood species and treatment:**

| Category | Species / Treatment | Ground Contact OK? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Naturally durable | Redwood (heartwood only) | Yes | All-heart redwood; sapwood is NOT durable |
| Naturally durable | Western red cedar (heartwood) | Yes | Heartwood only; sapwood not durable |
| Naturally durable | Black locust | Yes | Very hard, excellent durability |
| Naturally durable | White oak | Yes | Good durability |
| Pressure-treated | Southern yellow pine, Douglas fir, hem-fir | Yes | Must comply with AWPA U1, Use Category 4B for ground contact |
| NOT durable | Spruce, pine (untreated), fir (untreated) | NO | Will rot in ground contact; use metal post base |

**CRC R304.1 / R304.4 requirements:**
- Wood used in ground contact or within 6 inches of earth must be naturally durable or preservative-treated
- Pressure-treated wood: AWPA U1, Commodity Specification A (sawn timber)
- Ground contact posts: Use Category 4B minimum
- Fasteners in contact with pressure-treated wood: hot-dipped galvanized, stainless steel, or silicon bronze
- Cuts and holes in treated wood must be field-treated with copper naphthenate or comparable preservative

**Standard lumber sizes for wood fences:**

| Component | Typical Size | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Posts | 4x4 (up to 6 ft fence) | Use 4x6 or 6x6 for tall fences or high-wind areas |
| Rails | 2x4 | Top and bottom rails; mid-rail for fences over 5 ft |
| Fence boards | 1x6, 1x4, 5/4x6 | Dog-ear, flat-top, or shaped top |
| Pickets | 1x3, 1x4 | Pointed, flat, or shaped top |

### Vinyl / PVC Fences

- Must be UV-stabilized for outdoor use
- Wind rating must be provided by manufacturer
- Posts are typically reinforced with internal aluminum or steel channel
- No ground-contact decay concern but posts must be set in concrete for stability
- ASTM F964 covers vinyl fence specifications
- Verify manufacturer's maximum wind speed rating matches project location

### Chain Link Fences

| Component | Specification |
|---|---|
| Fabric gauge | 9 gauge minimum (residential); 6 gauge (commercial/security) |
| Coating | Galvanized (ASTM A392) or vinyl-coated |
| Mesh opening | 2 inches standard; 1-3/4 inches max for pool barriers |
| Framework | Galvanized steel pipe; 1-5/8" OD line posts, 2-3/8" OD terminal/corner posts |
| Top rail | 1-3/8" OD galvanized pipe |
| Fittings | Galvanized: post caps, tension bands, brace bands, rail ends, tie wires |

**Pool barrier note:** Standard 2-inch chain link mesh does NOT comply with pool barrier requirements (4-inch sphere test passes, but the diagonal measurement of a 2-inch mesh opening can allow passage of a 4-inch sphere on the diagonal). Use 1-3/4 inch mesh or smaller for pool barriers. CBC 3119B.1 specifically limits chain link openings to 1-3/4 inches measured horizontally.

### Wrought Iron / Ornamental Metal Fences

- Picket spacing: maximum 4 inches between pickets (to prevent child passage — matches pool barrier 4-inch sphere test)
- Material: steel (galvanized or powder-coated), aluminum, or wrought iron
- Welded or mechanically fastened construction
- Posts: steel tube or pipe, set in concrete footings
- For pool barriers: no horizontal rails that could serve as ladder; vertical pickets only between top and bottom rails

## Property Line and Setback Guidance

### State Code
The California Building Code and Residential Code do **not** prescribe fence setbacks from property lines. This is entirely regulated at the city/county level through zoning ordinances.

### Common City Requirements (for reference only — always check city overlay)

| Zone / Location | Typical City Requirement |
|---|---|
| Front yard | 3-4 ft maximum height; 15-20 ft setback from front property line; or no fence in front setback |
| Side yard (interior) | 6 ft maximum height; 0 ft setback (on property line OK) |
| Rear yard | 6 ft maximum height; 0 ft setback (on property line OK) |
| Corner side yard | 3-4 ft max in sight triangle; 6 ft behind front setback line |
| Adjacent to alley | 6 ft max; may require setback from alley ROW |

### Property Line Best Practices
- Fence should be installed **entirely on the owner's property** (not straddling the property line) unless neighbors agree otherwise
- When fence is on or near property line, city may require a survey to verify location
- Encroachment into public right-of-way is generally prohibited and may require an encroachment permit

## Sight Distance Triangles

### State Code
The state building code does **not** prescribe sight distance triangles for fences. This is regulated by city traffic engineering standards and zoning ordinances.

### Typical City Requirements (for reference only)

Sight distance triangles ensure that drivers, pedestrians, and cyclists can see each other at intersections and driveways.

**Common standard:**
- **Triangle dimensions:** 10 ft x 10 ft from the intersection of curb lines (or edge of pavement)
- **Maximum height within triangle:** 30-36 inches above the curb or edge of pavement
- **Applies to:** Corner lots at street intersections, driveways at sidewalks/streets
- **Measured from:** Typically the curb face or edge of roadway pavement

**What must be kept clear:**
- Fences, walls, hedges, and structures within the sight triangle cannot exceed the height limit
- Applies to both permanent and temporary obstructions
- Trees with canopies above 7 ft and utility poles are typically exempt

## Fastener Requirements for Fences

| Environment | Fastener Type | Standard |
|---|---|---|
| Untreated wood | Galvanized or stainless steel | -- |
| Pressure-treated wood | Hot-dipped galvanized (HDG), stainless steel, or silicon bronze | ASTM A153 (HDG) |
| Coastal / salt air | Stainless steel (316 grade) | ASTM F593 |
| Metal-to-metal | Matching or more noble metal; stainless steel bolts | Avoid galvanic corrosion |

Electroplated (bright zinc) fasteners are NOT acceptable for outdoor or ground-contact use — they corrode rapidly. Hot-dipped galvanized is the minimum for exterior fence construction.
